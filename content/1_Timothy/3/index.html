recent convert

Or, "neophyte."

The Greek word translated "recent convert" (νεόφυτος) only appears in this verse in the New Testament. It literally means "newly planted," which can be seen in how the Septuagint uses it: Job 14:9; Psalm 128:3; Psalm 144:12; Isaiah 5:7.

The teaching of Jesus compares individuals who hear His word to the soil and resultant plants where the seed is sown (Luke 8:4–18; Matthew 15:13). When Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 3:6, "I planted, Apollos watered...," he is referring to the fact he was the first to teach the gospel in Corinth (see also 1 Corinthians 1:14–16).

How recent is "recent"? There are two ways to reckon.

One way is to keep the comparison to plants started in Jesus' teaching and implied by the word "neophyte." The difference between a new planting and a full-grown plant is fruit. If a plant produces fruit whose seed has produced viable plants, it is no longer a new planting but a mature plant. When a Christian can be shown to sow the seed that makes faithful Christians, he is no longer a neophyte.

The other way to reckon recency is to use Acts as a timeline for the appointment of elders at Ephesus.

The men who would become the elders at Ephesus obeyed the gospel in 19:5.

Acts 19:5

...They were baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus.

Paul summons the men who had gone on to become elders at Ephesus in 20:17.

Acts 20:17

...He sent to Ephesus and called the elders of the church to come to him.

The amount of time that passed between their initial obedience to the gospel and their summons in an official capacity must have been enough time to be considered "not a neophyte" (6).

Paul spent three years actually preaching in Ephesus.

Acts 19:8–10

And he entered the synagogue and for three months spoke boldly, reasoning and persuading them about the kingdom of God. But when some became stubborn and continued in unbelief, speaking evil of the Way before the congregation, he withdrew from them and took the disciples with him, reasoning daily in the hall of Tyrannus. This continued for two years, so that all the residents of Asia heard the word of the Lord, both Jews and Greeks.

Acts 19:21–22

Now after these events Paul resolved in the Spirit to pass through Macedonia and Achaia and go to Jerusalem, saying, "After I have been there, I must also see Rome." And having sent into Macedonia two of his helpers, Timothy and Erastus, he himself stayed in Asia for a while.

Acts 20:31

Therefore be alert, remembering that for three years I did not cease night or day to admonish every one with tears.

Paul departed Ephesus for Macedonia in 20:1, and he wrote the first letter to Timothy during this time.

Acts 20:1

...After encouraging them, he said farewell and departed for Macedonia.

1 Timothy 1:3

As I urged you when I was going to Macedonia, remain at Ephesus....

The instructions we here comment on in 1 Timothy 3 are for appointing the very same elders who would be summoned in Acts 20:17.

Acts 20:17

Now from Miletus he sent to Ephesus and called the elders of the church to come to him.

The time between Paul's departure from Ephesus in Acts 20:1 and Paul's summons from Miletus in 20:17 seems less than a year.

After departing Ephesus, he first passed through Macedonia and spent three months in Greece.

Acts 20:2–3

When he had gone through those regions and had given them much encouragement, he came to Greece. There he spent three months....

After this, Paul hurried to get to Jerusalem before Pentecost--a goal he had set while still in Ephesus.

Acts 19:21

Now after these events Paul resolved in the Spirit to pass through Macedonia and Achaia and go to Jerusalem....

Acts 20:16

Paul had decided to sail past Ephesus, so that he might not have to spend time in Asia, for he was hastening to be at Jerusalem, if possible, on the day of Pentecost.

This timeline from Acts has Timothy appointing men who had obeyed the gospel a little over three years ago.

Some have suggested a man sheds his neophyte qualities only after many, many years--even 30 years. (Contrast Hebrews 5:11–14.) This assertion is entirely unbiblical. Any proposed amount of time must fit between Acts 19:5 and Acts 20:17.
